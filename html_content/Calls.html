If I asked you “what sounds do birds make?”, I bet the first sounds that comes to mind are their beautiful songs. However, birds not only sing, but have a variety of different calls too.

Communicating about predators is very beneficial to survival. Not only does it warn relatives about nearby danger and help keep them safe, but it can also provoke nearby birds to harass or attack predators till move on and become less of a threat. It can also tell the predator that they have been spotted, so any ambush attempts would be pointless. The calls that birds use to warn others about predators we call alarm calls.

Alarm calls come in a variety of types and different alarm calls can communicate different types of information. For example, particular calls can communicate the type of predator present, the level of danger it poses, whether it’s moving, and even its size. Intriguingly, some bird species only appear to have a single type of alarm call that they use towards all types of predator and threats (a general alarm call), whereas others species appear to lack alarm calls altogether. We don’t yet know why particular bird species differ in the types of alarm calls they use.

Bird alarm calls can often be grouped into two broad categories. Aerial alarm calls are used towards aerial threats, such as predatory birds in flight. These calls provoke other birds to either scan the sky to locate the predator or to flee into bushes and trees to stay hidden. Mobbing alarm calls are used towards ground predators like snakes and foxes but also perched predatory birds. These calls usually provoke other birds to seek out and inspect the location of danger, and if they see a predator, they may begin to harass or attack it.

Some birds have very complex alarm communication systems, such as the Japanese great tit. Japanese great tits are small birds that build their nests in tree cavities or human-made nest boxes. Their nestlings are vulnerable to a range of different types of predators, including crows, snakes, and martens (weasel-like mammals). When there is a predator near their nest, great tit parents use different alarm calls, or adjust how frequently they call, depending on the type of predator present. For example, they give ‘chika’ calls if a crow is nearby and ‘jar’ calls if a snake is nearby. In turn, great tit nestlings respond differently when they hear the different alarm calls, crouching when they hear ‘chika’ alarms and jumping out of the nest when they hear ‘jar’.

Japanese great tits use different alarm calls to different predators

An interesting phenomenon is that birds not only understand and respond to alarm calls of their own species, but often recognize the alarm calls of other local species. This behaviour is a type of eavesdropping as other species are often not the intended audience of these alarms. Eavesdropping on other species' alarm calls is widespread in nature and not just common to birds. For example, monkeys have been found to listen in on an alarm calls of other monkey species and local birds. Even a species of lizard has even been found to respond to alarm calls of a local bird.

But how do birds recognize the alarm calls of other species? There are two prevailing ideas and both are both supported by scientific evidence. The first idea is that different species' alarm calls sound similar to each other, or share particular features such as being high pitched, and therefore can be easily recognized. Although there is evidence of that this is the case in some circumstances, alarm calls of different species often sound quite different, and birds may not automatically respond to another species' alarm call if they are unfamiliar with that species' calls. The second idea is that birds learn to recognize alarm calls of other species.

My colleagues and I tested this second idea using small Australian birds called superb fairy-wren as our test subjects. Fairy-wrens are small birds that usually feed on the ground and in the open. They are very good at recognizing and responding to familiar species' alarm calls. We conducted an experiment to test if we could teach fairy-wrens to recognize “new” types of alarm calls. In our experiment, we designed and broadcast “new” types of alarm calls to wild fairy-wrens. These “new” alarms were broadcast either in combination with a fake gliding sparrow-hawk or alone. Although the fairy-wrens initially ignored the calls, after they heard the calls in combination with our gliding hawks a few times, they began treating these calls like any other aerial alarm call. On the other hand, they ignored the calls if the calls were not broadcast together with a gliding hawk.

Why should we care about bird calls or eavesdropping?

This can be a tough question to answer when asked by someone with no interest in the natural world, and is at heart of explaining why basic science and documenting natural history are important. On one hand, one can argue that is fundamentally important to know about the world around us and the mysteries and complexities it holds. If you’ve ever watched a David Attenborough documentary, you probably know that simply by observing and learning about nature, we come to appreciate it a lot more. On another level, figuring out how and why animals communicate helps us understand how complex communication systems evolve, and in some way brings us closer to understanding why we as humans have evolved the most complex vocal communication of all living animals. We also can never know whether basic scientific discoveries have potential future applications. For example, the knowledge we have gained from uncovering how birds recognize other species alarm calls can be used to help teach endangered and captive-bred species to recognize alarm calls of the local species at release sites to boost their chances of survival.
